Under the hood: why it stays light

Other tools hand your AI the whole raw page — all the markup, menus and scripts, or a giant behind-the-scenes "map" of the page. That's enormous and mostly noise. Handle pulls out only the part you'd actually read and hands over clean, tidy text (Markdown). Same page, a tiny fraction of the size.

Other tools — the raw page~50,000 words of code
Handle — just the content, tidied up~800

A typical page, give or take — roughly 95%+ less for your AI to chew on. Cheaper, faster, and it reads the real content instead of wading through menus and code (which is also where AI tools get confused).

How it actually gets the content

Behind one plain "read the tab," Handle tries the cleanest, cheapest way first — and only drops to the next if it has to. You always get usable content, or a clear reason why not.

  1. A snapshot it already hasIf it captured the page a moment ago, it reuses that — instant, nothing to re-fetch.
  2. The live page — content onlyReads the page and keeps just the article: headings, lists, links, tables, turned into clean Markdown. The menus, ads and footer are dropped.
  3. The document's own copyFor Google Docs & Sheets, and Word files on SharePoint, it pulls the real document — straight from your signed-in session — as tidy text or a table.
  4. A picture, read with visionFor designs, PDFs, or anything with no readable text, it takes a screenshot and your AI reads the image.
  5. Your clipboardLast resort — you copy the page, it grabs what's on your clipboard. There's always a way through.

The clever part: it reads your real browser

Most "AI that browses" tools open a fresh, robot-controlled Chrome that's signed into nothing — a stranger to your actual work. Handle does the opposite: it reads the browser you're already logged into. Programs are walled off from your browser by design, so Handle keeps several ways in and tries the smoothest one first, quietly dropping to the next if it isn't available.

  1. The browser add-on — preferredA quiet helper inside Chrome itself. No fuss, sees your real tabs and groups, takes clean screenshots, and even wakes a tab that fell asleep before reading it.
  2. The built-in messenger — always thereAsks your Mac to relay to Chrome. Nothing to install, so it's the floor that's always available — just a little clumsier.
  3. The developer hatch — opt-inA precise back-channel you unlock on purpose. Most exact, and it never grabs the wrong window.

Because it always has a backup way in, a read almost never just fails — if the best door is locked, it uses the next. That's the whole trick.

What makes it different

Five things, in one breath:

  1. Your real, signed-in browserIt sees what's behind your logins — not a stranger's empty Chrome.
  2. Several ways in, tried best-firstNo single point of failure; it degrades gracefully instead of breaking.
  3. A picture as a fallbackEven a design or PDF with no text is still readable — your AI looks at a screenshot.
  4. It reads your browser's own memoryHistory, downloads, closed tabs — not just what's open right now.
  5. It only ever looksNever clicks, types, or buys — and it all stays on your machine.

Set it up once

You'll need macOS, Google Chrome, an AI assistant you already use (like Claude Code), and Node + Python 3 for the local bridge (most dev machines already have both). It's free and open source, runs entirely on your own computer — no account, no API key. It reads your tabs; it never sends them anywhere, and it can't touch anyone else's.

1

Get it

git clone https://github.com/abhitsian/handle.git ~/claude-apps/handle
2

Connect it to your AI

One line, and your assistant can read your tabs:

claude mcp add handle -- node ~/claude-apps/handle/mcp/server.js
3

Let it read your pages — two one-time settings

a. The first read pops a macOS prompt to let it control Chrome — click OK.
b. In Chrome, turn on View → Developer → Allow JavaScript from Apple Events. This one's a menu toggle, not a popup, and it's off by default — it's what lets Handle read the actual text on a page. Skip it and tabs list fine but reads come back empty.

4

Just talk to it

"Summarize the doc I'm looking at."   "What does the figma show?"   "Answer this from my open tabs."   That's the whole thing.
